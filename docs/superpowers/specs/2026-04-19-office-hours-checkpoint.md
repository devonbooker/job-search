### 1. Devon has an unfair advantage he's not using

Most pre-launch founders have zero of these. Devon has all three:

- He IS the target user (actively job-hunting in security/infra in SF)
- Lives in the highest-density market for technical job seekers on Earth

The compound asset isn't the code — it's network + domain credibility. **It depreciates every week he doesn't use it,** because he'll graduate the persona once he lands a role and stops being a job seeker.

### 2. The wedge that has a real shot is narrower than what's been built

Not "interview prep for job seekers." Not even "interview prep for engineers." The hypothesis worth testing:

> **End-to-end job-search co-pilot for security/infra engineers actively interviewing at SF startups.**

Why this wedge:

"How do I make this a better product" assumes there IS a product. Today there's a prototype with one user. The right question for the next 30 days is:

> **"Who are the 5 people whose feedback I trust most, and what do they say after I watch them try it?"**

Not "what do I build next." Not "should I pivot to interview prep."

## Anti-patterns to avoid before next session

- Don't keep building features. Even great ones. Each new feature without external feedback widens the gap between what you think users want and what they actually want.
- Don't pivot. "Maybe I should make it interview-only" is a feature decision masquerading as a pivot. Don't decide before talking to anyone.
- Don't redesign the UI based on your own taste. Same reason.
- DO open the codebase only for: bugs that block someone from trying it (when you have someone to try it).
